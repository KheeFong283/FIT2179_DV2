Malaysia Crime Data Analysis (2016-2023)

Exploring crime patterns across states, time, and society

Is Malaysia Truly Safe?

At first glance, Malaysia appears relatively safe when compared to many ASEAN countries based on the Global Peace Index (GPI). However, this ranking is an aggregated measure that combines multiple dimensions such as societal safety, ongoing conflict, and militarisation.

While Malaysia may score better in overall, a higher GPI ranking does not automatically mean it is “safe” in everyday lived experience.

Types of Crime in Malaysia

When breaking down crime by type, vehicle theft emerges as the most common offence. Motorcycle theft, motorcar thft and lorry theft, which together form a substantial share of overall crime.

The dataset also includes serious offences such as murder, rape, break in, and cases involving causing injury.

Low in Proportion, But Too Serious to Overlook

Although crimes such as murder and rape represent a small proportion of total crime types, they should never be normalised or overlooked simply because they occur less frequently.

An annual range of 200-500 murders and 1,500-2,000 rape cases translates into thousands of lives directly affected each year. Even if statistically “low,” these figures remain deeply unsettling and highlight the severe human impact behind the data.

Crime Count Rankings

When observing crime trends across states over time, one key pattern stands out: the relative rankings remain largely unchanged.

Selangor consistently records the highest number of crimes throughout the period, maintaining its position as the most crime-prone state in the dataset. In contrast, Perlis consistently ranks among the lowest in crime counts.

A Positive Shift: Overall Crime in Malaysia Has Been Declining

On a more encouraging note, Malaysia has shown a consistent downward trend in overall crime from 2016 to 2023, including both urban and rural areas.

While urban crime has decreased steadily, rural areas show a much smaller decline, indicating that improvements have been less pronounced outside major cities.

Conclusion

Malaysia's crime shows a clear long-term decline from 2016 to 2023, followed by a period of relative stabilisation in recent years, where overall crime levels fluctuate within a narrower range rather than continuing to fall sharply. This suggests that while major improvements have been achieved, further reductions may be slower and more incremental.

Across all categories, vehicle-related theft, especially motorcycle and motorcar theft — consistently dominates crime types, forming the largest share of reported offences. This indicates that property-related crimes remain the most persistent challenge in the dataset.

While serious offences such as murder and rape make up a small share of total cases, the absolute numbers remain concerning over time, still reaching hundreds to thousands of cases annually. Although they do not dominate overall trends, their persistence shows that reductions in total crime do not always lead to proportional declines in violent crime.

Overall, state rankings and crime patterns stay quite stable over time, suggesting they are driven more by long-term factors like urbanisation and population differences rather than short-term changes or temporary factors.
