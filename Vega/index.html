Malaysia Crime Data Analysis

Is Malaysia Truly Safe?

At first glance, Malaysia appears relatively safe when compared to many ASEAN countries based on the Global Peace Index (GPI). However, this ranking is an aggregated measure that combines multiple dimensions such as societal safety, ongoing conflict, and militarisation.

While Malaysia may score better in overall, a higher GPI ranking does not automatically mean it is “safe” in everyday lived experience.

How Safe is Malaysia?

This choropleth map visualises the distribution of recorded crimes across different states in Malaysia from 2016 - 2023. It allows us to identify how crime levels vary geographically rather than relying on a single national figure.

On average, Malaysia records approximately 76,000 crimes per year. However, this total is not evenly distributed, with some states consistently reporting higher numbers than others.

Among all states, Selangor records the highest number of crimes compared to other regions.

What Drives Crime in Each State?

After examining the total number of crimes by state, a clearer pattern emerges when we break down the dominant crime types within each region.

Across most states, motorcycle theft and other forms of theft consistently dominate the crime profile, making up the largest share of reported incidents regardless of location.

This suggests that crime in Malaysia is largely driven by opportunistic property-related offences rather than violent crime, though the distribution and intensity still vary between states.

Types of Crime in Malaysia

When breaking down crime by type, vehicle theft emerges as the most common offence, accounting for the largest proportion of reported cases. This is closely followed by other theft-related offences, including motorcycle theft and lorry theft, which together form a substantial share of overall crime.

Beyond property-related crimes, the dataset also includes serious offences such as murder, rape, break in, and cases involving causing injury.

Low in Proportion, But Too Serious to Overlook

Although crimes such as murder and rape represent a small proportion of total crime types, they should never be normalised or overlooked simply because they occur less frequently.

An annual range of 200–500 murders and 1,500–2,000 rape cases translates into thousands of lives directly affected each year. Even if statistically “low,” these figures remain deeply unsettling and highlight the severe human impact behind the data.

Gender Gap in Incarceration: Male Prisoners Dominate

When examining prison population by gender, a clear imbalance emerges, with male prisoners making up a significantly larger proportion compared to females.

This pattern is not just a numerical difference, but also reflects broader social and behavioural factors. Crime types that dominate the dataset—particularly property offences and violent incidents—are statistically more likely to involve male offenders, which contributes to the consistently higher incarceration rates.

As a result, female prisoners represent only a small fraction of the total prison population, highlighting how criminal behaviour and enforcement outcomes differ significantly across gender groups.

Crime Trends Across States: Stable Rankings Over Time

When observing crime trends across states over time, one key pattern stands out: the relative rankings remain largely unchanged.

Selangor consistently records the highest number of crimes throughout the period, maintaining its position as the most crime-prone state in the dataset. In contrast, Perlis consistently ranks among the lowest in crime counts.

A Positive Shift: Overall Crime in Malaysia Has Been Declining

On a more encouraging note, Malaysia has shown a consistent downward trend in overall crime from 2016 to 2023.

This sustained decline suggests improvements in law enforcement effectiveness, preventive measures, and broader social conditions over time, even though the distribution of crime across states remains relatively stable.

While challenges still exist, the long-term trend provides a positive signal that overall safety conditions in the country are gradually improving.
